Design books tend to be a bit navel-gazey. They often make observations about the nature of design work that rarely extend outside the authors own context — leading to [overhyped principles](https://ghostly.com/products/vitsoe-dieter-rams-10-principles-for-good-design-poster).

A rare exception, though, is Christopher Alexander's _Notes on the Synthesis of Form_. Published in 1964, its ideas feel as relevant today as ever.

In it he shares a model for thinking about design in almost algebraic terms. He doesn't take stances on aesthetics or function, but instead provides some wider frameworks for evaluating the qualities of a designed thing.

One of his best ideas — which I haven't seen talked about on the web — is his notion of _fit:_ good fit, bad fit, and how to evaluate it.

### Form, context, and fit

According to Alexander, any designed object is a response to one's environment.

From the earliest human tools, ceramics, and buildings, to the mass manufactured goods, graphic works, and software of today — all of these things seek, in some small way, to alter our relationship with the world.

A clay pot makes it easier to store goods. A kettle helps boil water in a fast, safe way. A website for an art gallery reflects the reality of their audience’s online attention, questions, and desire for info before they venture out into the world and the gallery's own desire to be seen and known. Whether a pot, kettle, or website, the "success" of an object can be thought of how well its form is suited to its context. Does it have the effect on us and the world that we want?

We can think about the **form of an object** as the entirety of its qualities. Its shape, size, weight, color, texture, and smell, but also its cost, production methods, flammability, longevity, and so on.

**Its context** is the the innummerable, ever-changing, swirling set of reality the object finds itself in. Where it's being used, the people using it, the ambient temperature, the point in time, the aesthetic preferences of its audience, the cultural values it sits in, etc.

And so, **fit is the relationship between the form and the context.** It's the ensemble of the two.

<GoodFitRadial width={900} height={640} />

For example, you could _try_ to use a clay pot to eat soup, but it wouldn't go well — it's shape, size, and weight make it hard to hold. And how it'd fit in your mouth is a bit of an open question.

On the other hand, a spoon just works. A spoon has every quality we'd want when eating soup: it's easy to hold in our hands, it's easy to fit in our mouths, it's light and easy to move. And they're cheap; we can buy spoons for our whole family! Soup for everyone!

{/* Usually, when we talk about an object, we're talking not about its form, but about the ensemble. An imagined context is baked into our words — its hard to imagine a spoon without also imagining its use. */}

We can say that good fit is when form and context are in effortless contact. It's the subtle _it does what I want, in the way that I want it to_ feeling you get from a useful and novel kitchen tool or well-designed piece of software.

Bad fit, on the other hand, is when the form and context are in conflict.

### Factors of fit

One of the trickiest parts of this equation (and design generally) is **defining the context a thing sits in**.

As I mentioned above, contexts are almost infinite. Even describing a simple street corner can take [60 pages of writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An_Attempt_at_Exhausting_a_Place_in_Paris), let alone the multitude of ways people could use and abuse a kettle.

We can only begin to talk about contexts by scoping them down to generalized sets[^1] of factors, often in the form of questions:

- How well does the spoon hold liquids?
- How well does the spoon fit in one's hand?
- How well does the spoon fit in one's mouth?

For a spoon to be well-designed, its form needs good answers to each question posed by the context. A spoon which does not hold liquids well is certainly not a good spoon. One that holds liquids but doesn't fit in a mouth might be better suited as a ladle than as a spoon. A spoon that fits in a baby's mouth is not necessarily good for an adult.

Looking more broadly, we can also think of other factors that play into the context that go beyond the spoon's function or ergonomics:

- How easy is it to manufacture?
- How cheaply can it be produced?
- How long does it last before it degrades?
- Can the material safely be put in one's mouth?
- Will the spoon melt if exposed to high temperatures?

Starting to list out all the factors that go into a design can be an enlightening look into the scope of what goes into an ordinary object.

These factors, and many others that haven't been listed, all work together to create a larger picture of "good fit." The fewer factors where the form doesn't meet the context, the better the fit.

### Relationships between factors of "fit"

So, good fit relies on finding fit across many different factors. But it's not as easy as "checking all the boxes."

Finding fit in one factor often has a negative impact on another. For example:

- Designing a spoon, choosing a cheaper plastic can keep costs low, but may end up having a slight taste, or break down quickly, or have a non-ideal mouthfeel.
- Designing a poster layout, you may want to make some text more prominent by moving it higher on the page. But that shift can result in the text being too close to another piece of text, potentially resulting in a confusing reading. Or, it may result in a poor flow of the eye across the page, demanding that other objects must also be moved.

One move results in needing to shuffle the whole arrangement around until you find something that fits.

Just like we can try to map out all the factors of fit, we can also map relationships between them, like a network:

All design is a kind of layout-solving problem — finding an equilibrium between factors that leads to a good fit.

This is why [more time results in better work](https://elezea.com/2013/01/no-time/). More time means more wildly different variations, and then more minor iterations to refine the balance you strike between factors. You can prototype your way to a global maximum.

It's also explains why design principles are unreasonably effective tools: good principles clarify which relationships to prioritize over others. A principle like [Medium's _Direction over Choice_](https://medium.com/@dustin/thanks-for-writing-the-article-julie-8362fd235ae0) embeds a strong opinion on how to trade off different aspects of fit — don't bother appealing to everyone with customization options, and instead focus on well-crafted, single-style editorial typography.

You optimize for the important criteria and others fall on a sliding scale of success.

### Visualizing fit

Equipped with this mental model, Alexander proposes a little experiment:

> Imagine a system of a hundred lights.
>
> Each light can be in one of two possible states. In one state the light is on. The lights are so constructed that any light which is on always has a 50-50 chance of going off in the next second.
>
> In the other state the light is off. Connections between lights are constructed so that any light which is off has a 50-50 chance of going on again in the next second, provided at least one of the lights it is connected to is on.
>
> If the lights it is directly connected to are off, for the time being it has no chance of going on again, and stays off. If the lights are ever all off simultaneously, then they will all stay off for good, since when no light is on, none of the lights has any chance of being reactivated. This is a state of equilibrium. Sooner or later the system of lights will reach it.

It's a verbose description, but we can distill it down to a few key points:

1. Lights have two states: on or off
1. Lights are connected to other lights
1. Every second, a light has a 50% chance of changing from on to off, or off to on
1. Lights which only connect to "off" lights has no chance of turning on

Each light represents a factor of fit: whether or not the spoon fits well in the average mouth, if the material is safe or not, etc, etc.

And the state of each light represents its "fit." Lights which are off have achieved good fit, lights that are on haven't. And when all the related factors have found good fit, they are no longer at risk of losing it. The state of equilibrium, when all lights are off, represents perfect fit or adaptation to the context.

If it hasn't already come to mind from talking about variables, networks, connections — this sounds a lot like a [force-directed graph](https://github.com/d3/d3-force).

### Achieving good fit

Now, it can be fun to watch the lights flicker on and off, but there's more to be gleaned from this experiment. By playing around with the number of connections, you can notice patterns.

Systems with many factors but few connections between the factors resolve themselves quickly:

On the other hand, arrangements with fewer factors but many connections between the factors stay unresolved for quite some time:

<GoodFitVisualization variant="many-connections" tickMs={50} />

In an ideal world, we would work on design problems that look like the system with few connections. A world where the size of a spoon doesn't affect its cost, or its shape doesn't affect the complexity of manufacturing.

And indeed, every once in a while, new technologies are developed that remove these complicated relationships — like how digital printing made it (roughly) as cost-efficient to print with many colors as with one, or how it made it cheap to print less than a few thousand units.

Of course, we still regularly deal with problems that have lots of interconnected factors.

Alexander proposes a solution. Consider this diagram, which has the same number of factors and connections as the previous diagram.

<GoodFitVisualization variant="groups" tickMs={50} />

This diagram shows the same number of connections, but rather than one big cluster, factors are broken into several smaller groups.

The interesting thing here is that despite having the same number of factors and connections as the high-connection diagram, the system resolves itself as quickly as the low-connection one.

In software design, this principle is known as ["loose coupling"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loose_coupling), and people go through great efforts to achieve it. Loosely coupled systems are usually easy to change and adapt over time. By isolating complicated relationships into small "sub-systems" or "sub-problems", it becomes much easier to acheive a sense of good fit. As long as the subsystems are _actually_ independent (and don't just seem like they are), we can iterate towards good solutions at a quicker pace. We can tackle complex problems in one sub-system without affecting work done in others.

I've experienced this in small ways designing screens for mobile applications. I'd spend days trying to refine all the information and actions which need to live on one page. I'd try different visual arrangements or interactions to not overload people with detail but still keep important things accessible. Then, someone would suggest just putting some of the information on an entirely separate screen altogether. Zing! It works. By isolating out the key concepts, it not only made both pages easier to manage and design in isolation, but also opened up the possibility to use one of those pages in other contexts.

### On Mis-Fit

One more interesting note about fit.

When we think about good fit, there's no single quality or property of a form we can point to that makes it suitable for a context. **What characterizes good fit is simply the absence of bad fit**.

Unfortunately, this means that it's almost impossible to describe what good fit looks like in a new context. You can't know on first glance! What works in one place could be an abject failure in another.

Looking at a kitchen, it may be easy to have a gut reaction about whether or not it's well-made. But beyond the aesthetic and some sense of "cultural fit" (does it embody status?), it's hard to say it's well designed until you live in it a little bit. You may quickly discover it's a little hard to clean, and that food keeps falling between the cracks of the stove and the counter, and the materials pick up stains easily, and the cupboard meant for spices is in an awkward spot.

A good kitchen is simply one where those little points of friction don't exist.

But it's hard to think in negative terms.

However, it's hard to speak in negative terms.

> It is the departure from norms that stand out in our minds rather than the norm itself. Their wrongness is somehow more immediate than the rightness of less peculiar behavior, and therefore more compelling . Whenever an instance of a misfit occurs, we are able to point specifically at what fails to describe it. It seems as though in practice the concept of good fit can only be explained indirectly.

- **Sharing archetypes and design patterns**. "When you have a problem that has this kind of shape, this kind of solution tends to work."
- **Iterating over time**. Experiencing the points of mis-fit and then trying to solve them.
- **Relying on critiques**. Having other people point out the mis-fits in your work.

### More notes

I find this mental model useful for thinking about my own work in very pragmatic terms.

Some lessons that follow from this way of thinking:

-
- The smaller the context, or the more sub-systems contexts are broken down into, the easier it is to find good fit. This is probably why it's easy to have a "finished" CLI tool but much much harder to have a "finished" web app.

I'd encourage you to read _Notes on the Synthesis of Form_, and Alexander's work more generally. If you'd like a wider peek into the book, [my Are.na channel on it](https://www.are.na/ross-zurowski/reading-notes-on-the-synthesis-of-form) has a bunch more notes that may be of interest.
