Christopher Alexander's _Notes on the Synthesis of Form_ is one of my favourite design books. Published in 1964, it's a collection of notes and writing from his architecture practice, sharing mental models for thinking about design work that he's collected over his career.

He covers lots of topics, but one of his best ideas — which I haven't seen talked about on the web — is his notion of _fit:_ good fit, bad fit, and how to evaluate it.

### Form, context, and fit

We can think of any designed object as a response to one's environment.

From the earliest human tools, ceramics, and buildings, to the mass manufactured goods, graphic works, and software of today — all of these things seek, in some small way, to alter our relationship with the world.

A clay pot makes it easier to store goods. A kettle helps boil water in a fast, safe way. A website for an art gallery reflects the reality of their audience’s online attention, questions, and desire for info before they venture out into the world and the gallery's own desire to be seen and known. Whether a pot, kettle, or website, the "success" of an object can be thought of how well its form is suited to its context. Does it have the effect on us and the world that we want?

We can think about the **form of an object** as the entirety of its qualities. Its shape, size, weight, color, texture, and smell, but also its cost, production methods, flammability, longevity, and so on.

**An object's context** is the the ever-changing reality it finds itself in. Where it's being used, the people using it, the ambient temperature, the point in time, the aesthetic preferences of its audience, the cultural values it sits in, etc.

When we speak of design, what we're really talking about isn't the form itself, nor the context, but the assemblage of the two, and how they relate to each other. And so, **fit is the relationship between the form and the context.**

<GoodFitRadial width={900} height={640} />

For example, you could _try_ to use a clay pot to eat soup, but it wouldn't go well — its shape, size, and weight make it hard to hold. And how it'd fit in your mouth is a bit of an open question.

A spoon, on the other hand, just works. Spoons have every quality we'd want when eating soup: they're easy to hold in our hands, they're easy to fit in our mouths, they're light and easy to move. And they're cheap; we can buy spoons for our whole family! Soup for everyone!

{/* Usually, when we talk about an object, we're talking not about its form, but about the ensemble. An imagined context is baked into our words — its hard to imagine a spoon without also imagining its use. */}

When form and context are in a kind of "effortless contact" with each other, that's good fit. It's the subtle _it does what I want, in the way that I want it to_ feeling you get from a useful and novel kitchen tool or well-designed piece of software. Bad fit, on the other hand, is when the two are in conflict.

{/* While there's no objective good or objective bad, within a particular frame, a particular context, there is. */}

### Factors of fit

One of the trickiest parts of this equation (and design generally) is **defining the context a thing sits in**.

Contexts are practically infinite. Even describing a simple street corner can take [60 pages of writing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/An_Attempt_at_Exhausting_a_Place_in_Paris), let alone the multitude of ways people could use and abuse a kettle.

We can only begin to talk about contexts by scoping them down to generalized sets[^1] of factors, often in the form of questions:

- How well does the spoon hold liquids?
- How well does the spoon fit in one's hand?
- How well does the spoon fit in one's mouth?

Looking more broadly, we can also think of other factors that play into the context that go beyond the spoon's function or ergonomics:

- How easy is it to manufacture?
- How cheaply can it be produced?
- How long does it last before it degrades?
- Can the material safely be put in one's mouth?
- Will the spoon melt if exposed to high temperatures?

For a spoon to be well-designed, its form needs good answers to each question posed by the context. A spoon which does not hold liquids well is certainly not a good spoon. One that holds liquids but doesn't fit in a mouth might be better suited as a ladle than as a spoon. A spoon that fits in a baby's mouth is not necessarily good for an adult.

You could imagine (with great effort) an attempt to exhaust all the possible factors that go into a kettle's fit:

While some factors may have an easily measurable standard (eg. cost per unit, maintenance cost per year, operation volume in decibels), just as many are missing a concrete scale. Just because "comfort to hold" or "sense of warmth and coziness" are hard to quantify doesn't make them any less real as factors.

These factors, and many others that haven't been listed, all work together to create a larger picture of "good fit." The fewer factors where the form doesn't meet the context, the better the fit.

### Relationships between factors of "fit"

Of course, good fit isn't as easy as "checking all the boxes," even if you can ennumerate them all.

Finding fit in one factor often has a negative impact on another. For example:

- When designing a spoon, choosing a cheaper plastic can keep costs low, but may end up having a slight taste, or break down quickly, or have a non-ideal mouthfeel.
- When designing a poster layout, you may want to make some text more prominent by moving it higher on the page. But that shift can result in the text being too close to another piece of text, potentially resulting in a confusing reading. Or, it may result in a poor flow of the eye across the page, demanding that other objects must also be moved.

One change can result in needing to shuffle the whole arrangement around to make it work.

You can imagine, like trying to map out all the factors of fit, we can also map relationships between them, like a network:

Thinking this way, we could see design as a kind of layout-solving problem. We're trying to find an equilibrium between factors that leads to a good fit.

This is why [more time results in better work](https://elezea.com/2013/01/no-time/). More time means more wildly different variations, and then more minor iterations to refine the balance you strike between factors.

It's also explains why design principles are unreasonably effective tools: good principles clarify which relationships to prioritize over others. A principle like [Medium's _Direction over Choice_](https://medium.com/@dustin/thanks-for-writing-the-article-julie-8362fd235ae0) embeds a strong opinion on how to trade off different aspects of fit — don't bother appealing to everyone with customization options, and instead focus on well-crafted, single-style editorial typography.

You optimize for the important criteria and others fall on a sliding scale of success.

### Visualizing relationships of fit

Now for a fun little experiment.

Equipped with this mental model, Alexander suggests we could see how these networks work together like so:

> Imagine a system of a hundred lights.
>
> Each light can be in one of two possible states. In one state the light is on. The lights are so constructed that any light which is on always has a 50-50 chance of going off in the next second.
>
> In the other state the light is off. Connections between lights are constructed so that any light which is off has a 50-50 chance of going on again in the next second, provided at least one of the lights it is connected to is on.
>
> If the lights it is directly connected to are off, for the time being it has no chance of going on again, and stays off. If the lights are ever all off simultaneously, then they will all stay off for good, since when no light is on, none of the lights has any chance of being reactivated. This is a state of equilibrium. Sooner or later the system of lights will reach it.

We can distill it down to a few key points:

1. Lights have two states: on or off.
1. Lights are connected to other lights.
1. Every second, a light has a 50% chance of changing from on to off, or off to on.
1. Lights which only connect to "off" lights has no chance of turning on.

Each light represents a factor of fit: whether or not the spoon fits well in the average mouth, if the material is safe or not, etc, etc.

Whether the light is on or off represents its fit. Lights which are off have achieved good fit, lights that are on haven't. And when all the related factors have found good fit, they are no longer at risk of losing it. The state of equilibrium, when all lights are off, represents perfect fit or adaptation to the context.

Eventually though, with time and iteration, we settle on a state of equilibrium.

### Achieving good fit

Now, it can be fun to watch the lights flicker on and off, but there's more to be gleaned from this experiment. We can play around with the number of connections to find patterns.

For example, systems with many factors but few connections between the factors resolve themselves quickly. (I usually see between 5–20 iterations)

Conversely, arrangements with many connections between factors them stay unresolved for quite some time:

<GoodFitVisualization variant="many-connections" tickMs={50} />

In an ideal world, we would work on problems that look like the system with few connections. A world where the size of a spoon doesn't affect its cost, or its shape doesn't affect the complexity of manufacturing.

And every once in a while, new technologies are developed that remove these complicated relationships. The introduction of digital printing made it (roughly) as cost-efficient to print with many colors as with one, or how it made it cheap to print less than a few thousand units. Trade-offs still exist, but not as prominently.

However, there's another way to deal with tricky problems. Consider this diagram:

<GoodFitVisualization variant="groups" tickMs={50} />

It has the same number of factors and connections as the previous one, but broken into several smaller groups. What's more — **this system often resolves itself as quickly as the low-connection one.**

This approach can be thought of as ["loose coupling"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loose_coupling), or simply "breaking a big problem down into smaller ones", but this shows how effective of a technique it is.

By isolating complicated relationships into small "sub-systems" or "sub-problems", it becomes much easier to acheive a sense of good fit. As long as the subsystems are _actually_ independent (and don't just seem like they are), we can iterate towards good solutions at a quicker pace. We can tackle complex problems in one sub-system without affecting work done in others.

Some examples of this principle in play:

- In urban planning, rather than trying to think of city planning projects at a high-level, starting with individual streets and neighbourhoods and the character and nuance they embody.
- In software design, designing a component system, then building screens with those components, and apps with the set of screens.

Conversely, trying to split highly-related pieces into groups that aren't actually isolated is a recipe for disaster. In software development, the classic example is adopting "microservices," where every function is its own server/function. Teams quickly realize that they've put up communication barriers everywhere without actually simplifying the underlying relationships between factors of fit.

### Co-creating

Contexts also aren't static! They're constantly changing, and shaped themselves by the forms we respond to them with. Alexander puts it elegantly:

> A well-designed house not only fits its context well but also illuminates the problem of just what the context is, and thereby clarifies the life which it accommodates.

We learn about the context by making, and the context then shapes us in turn.

### On mis-fit

One more interesting property of fit. When we think about good fit, there's no single factor or quality we can point to that makes it suitable for a context.

Unfortunately, that makes good fit hard to talk about. It's almost impossible to describe good fit looks like in a new context. You can't know on first glance! What works in one place could be an abject failure in another. Instead, **what characterizes good fit is the _absence_ of bad fit**.

Looking at a kitchen interior, it may be easy to have a gut reaction about whether or not it's well-made. But beyond the aesthetic and some sense of "cultural fit" (does it embody status?), it's hard to say it's well-designed until you live in it a little bit. You may quickly discover it's a little hard to clean, and that food keeps falling between the cracks of the stove and the counter, and the materials pick up stains easily, and the cupboard meant for spices is in an awkward spot.

A good kitchen is simply one where those little points of friction don't exist.

Database schema design often falls into this category. It's very easy to make decisions and feel like they're the right ones. However, it's only months later, when you're fighting to `INNER JOIN` across 5 tables that you realize you wish you did things differently.

Articulating good fit as the absence of bad fit explains why these approaches are such effective tools:

- **Sharing archetypes, design patterns, examples**. "When you have a problem that has this kind of shape, this kind of solution tends to work."
- **Using critiques**. Having other people point out the mis-fits in your work that you might not have thought of.
- **Iterating over time**. Taking a guess at what good fit looks like, then experiencing the points of mis-fit and then trying to solve them.

### More notes

I find fit to be a useful bit of language for talking about my own work in pragmatic terms. I hope it serves you well too.

I'd encourage you to read _Notes on the Synthesis of Form_, and Alexander's work more generally. If you'd like a wider peek into the book, [my Are.na channel on it](https://www.are.na/ross-zurowski/reading-notes-on-the-synthesis-of-form) has a bunch more notes that may be of interest.
